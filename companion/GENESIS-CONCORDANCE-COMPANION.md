## 1) Purpose Thesis
Genesis lays the foundation of Scripture’s operating system: God creates with purpose, humanity breaks trust, and God launches a covenant promise that will bless the nations through a promised line. Genesis is not only “where things began”—it is where God establishes what the world is for, what went wrong, and how redemption will move forward through covenant faithfulness.

**If you miss this, you miss the book:** Genesis is the blueprint of **creation → rupture → promise → covenant line → preservation**.

---

## 2) Narrative Flow (Movements)
### Movement A — Creation and Calling (Gen 1–2)
God forms order by His word, blesses life, and appoints humanity as image-bearers to steward His world (Gen 1:26–28).

### Movement B — Rupture, Curse, and Seed Hope (Gen 3–5)
Trust fractures; the curse enters; violence escalates; yet hope is announced through seed promise logic (Gen 3:15).

### Movement C — Corruption and Reset (Gen 6–9)
Judgment confronts pervasive corruption, while mercy preserves the future through covenant stability (Gen 8:20–22; 9:8–17).

### Movement D — Nations, Pride, and Scattering (Gen 10–11)
Humanity seeks a name apart from God; scattering follows; the nations frame is set for mission (Gen 11:1–9).

### Movement E — Covenant Line Begins (Gen 12–25)
God calls Abraham; blessing is aimed outward to the nations; faith becomes the pathway of righteousness (Gen 12:1–3; 15:6).

### Movement F — The Line Continues Through Struggle (Gen 26–36)
Isaac and Jacob display weakness and transformation; God forms a people through wrestling and promise (Gen 32:24–30).

### Movement G — Providence Through Suffering (Gen 37–50)
Joseph’s suffering-to-glory arc preserves many; God turns evil toward preservation without calling evil good (Gen 50:20).

## 3) Core Theology (Genesis Themes)
1. **God as Creator-King** — God establishes order, goodness, and purpose (Gen 1–2).  
   *So what:* worship begins with God’s authority and goodness.

2. **Image of God** — human dignity and stewardship are foundational (Gen 1:26–28).  
   *So what:* people are never “tools”—they’re image-bearers.

3. **Boundary and Trust** — obedience flows from trust (Gen 2:16–17; 3).  
   *So what:* sin is not just rule-breaking; it’s relationship-breaking.

4. **Sin’s Spread** — violence and pride multiply (Gen 4; 6:5; 11:4).  
   *So what:* unchecked sin doesn’t stay private.

5. **Judgment and Mercy** — God confronts evil and preserves the future (Gen 6–9).  
   *So what:* God is not indifferent; He’s committed to justice and restoration.

6. **Covenant Framework** — God binds Himself to promise (Gen 12; 15; 17).  
   *So what:* redemption is covenant-shaped, not random.

7. **Faith and Righteousness** — faith is credited as righteousness (Gen 15:6).  
   *So what:* the pathway is trust, not self-salvation.

8. **Blessing for the Nations** — election is missional (Gen 12:1–3).  
   *So what:* being chosen is for serving others.

9. **God’s Patience with Weakness** — flawed people don’t cancel God’s promise (Gen 16–21; 27–32).  
   *So what:* God forms maturity over time.

10. **Providence** — God governs outcomes without authoring evil (Gen 45:5–8; 50:20).  
   *So what:* despair is not required when God is faithful.

## 4) Messiah Thread (Genesis as Anticipation)
- **Seed promise announced** (Gen 3:15) — pattern of ultimate victory over evil (see Rom 16:20).  
- **Nations-blessing covenant** (Gen 12:1–3) — doctrine lane for faith and mission (Gal 3:6–9).  
- **Provision/substitution pattern** (Gen 22) — God provides; faith obeys (Heb 11:17–19; Jas 2:21–23).  
- **Judah kingship horizon** (Gen 49:8–12) — kingship line that later becomes central (Rev 5:5).  
- **Joseph pattern** (Gen 45:5–8; 50:20) — rejected → exalted → many preserved (Acts 7:9–14).  
- **Eden/exile/return logic** (Gen 3:23–24) — restoration horizon (Rev 22:1–5).  
- **Babel scattering vs Spirit gathering** (Gen 11:1–9) — contrast lane (Acts 2).

## 6) Hard Questions (15)
1) **Is Genesis “science” or “theology”?**  
Genesis primarily establishes God’s identity, human purpose, and covenant foundations (Gen 1–2; 12; 15).  
Refs: Gen 1–2; Gen 12:1–3.  
Pastoral note: Don’t let debates steal the discipleship purpose.

2) **Why does God judge with the flood?**  
Genesis presents judgment as confronting pervasive corruption while preserving a future (Gen 6:5–8; 9:8–17).  
Refs: Gen 6:5–8; Gen 9:8–17.  
Pastoral note: God’s justice is not cruelty; it’s protection for restoration.

3) **What is the “seed” in Gen 3:15?**  
It’s a hope motif that the defeat of evil comes through a promised line; later Scripture frames ultimate victory in God’s final peace.  
Refs: Gen 3:15; Rom 16:20.  
Pastoral note: Hope starts early—God doesn’t abandon the story.

4) **Why choose Abraham?**  
Election is for blessing the nations, not superiority (Gen 12:1–3).  
Refs: Gen 12:1–3; Gen 18:19.  
Pastoral note: “Chosen” means “sent.”

5) **Why are patriarchs portrayed as flawed?**  
Genesis tells the truth: God’s promise survives human weakness to show grace and formation (Gen 27–32).  
Refs: Gen 27; Gen 32:24–30.  
Pastoral note: God can grow you without pretending you’re perfect.

6) **What does “faith counted as righteousness” mean?**  
Trust in God’s promise is credited as right-standing (Gen 15:6), later echoed as doctrine about faith.  
Refs: Gen 15:6; Gal 3:6.  
Pastoral note: You don’t earn God—He invites trust.

7) **Why is Gen 22 so intense?**  
It presents testing and provision; the story emphasizes God’s provision and the costliness of trust.  
Refs: Gen 22; Heb 11:17–19.  
Pastoral note: Testing is not abandonment—God refines trust.

8) **What’s the point of Babel?**  
It shows human unity used for self-glory and the resulting scattering; mission later reverses scattering by God’s Spirit.  
Refs: Gen 11:1–9; Acts 2.  
Pastoral note: Don’t chase a “name” at the expense of God’s way.

9) **How does Genesis connect to the rest of Scripture?**  
Genesis sets patterns: covenant, blessing, seed line, exile/return, suffering-to-glory.  
Refs: Gen 12; Gen 50:20; Gal 3:6–9.  
Pastoral note: The Bible is one story; Genesis sets the grammar.

10) **Is Joseph’s story just moral lessons?**  
It’s also providence: God preserves many through suffering and wisdom.  
Refs: Gen 45:5–8; Gen 50:20.  
Pastoral note: God can redeem seasons you didn’t choose.

11) **What does “image of God” change about ethics?**  
It grounds dignity, justice, and stewardship; people are not disposable.  
Refs: Gen 1:26–28; Gen 9:6.  
Pastoral note: Honor people when it’s costly—that’s Genesis ethics.

12) **Why does Genesis have genealogies?**  
They preserve the promise line and show how God carries history forward through families.  
Refs: Gen 5; Gen 10; Gen 11.  
Pastoral note: Your story is part of something bigger.

13) **Does Genesis teach that evil “wins”?**  
No—Genesis shows evil spreading, but God’s promise and preservation advance.  
Refs: Gen 3:15; Gen 50:20.  
Pastoral note: Don’t interpret delay as defeat.

14) **How should we read “blessing”?**  
Blessing is life that multiplies and travels outward—never hoarded.  
Refs: Gen 1:28; Gen 12:1–3.  
Pastoral note: Blessing is a lane for generosity.

15) **What is the main takeaway for daily life?**  
Trust God’s promise, resist Babel pride, practice blessing, and interpret life through providence.  
Refs: Gen 12:1–3; Gen 50:20.  
Pastoral note: Genesis is meant to be walked, not merely studied.

## 8) Walking Rule (Genesis Practices)

### Home (5)
1) **Creation gratitude** — name gifts daily; resist cynicism.  
Why: Genesis trains worship before complaint. Refs: Gen 1; Gen 2.

2) **Image-of-God speech** — speak dignity, not contempt.  
Why: words shape people; image-bearing matters. Refs: Gen 1:26–28.

3) **Boundary practice** — choose one joyful “limit” (time, money, media).  
Why: trust is trained through boundaries. Refs: Gen 2:16–17.

4) **Family blessing** — pray blessing over your household weekly.  
Why: blessing is meant to multiply life. Refs: Gen 1:28; Gen 12:2.

5) **Providence journal** — record “God carried us” moments.  
Why: interpretation shapes endurance. Refs: Gen 45:5–8; Gen 50:20.

### Church (5)
1) **Covenant faithfulness** — keep promises; apologize quickly.  
Why: covenant people reflect covenant God. Refs: Gen 17; Gen 18:19.

2) **Bless-the-nations posture** — serve outsiders intentionally.  
Why: election is mission-shaped. Refs: Gen 12:1–3.

3) **Resist Cain patterns** — address resentment early.  
Why: sin spreads when nursed. Refs: Gen 4.

4) **Practice reconciliation** — prioritize peace across conflict lines.  
Why: God preserves family for promise. Refs: Gen 33; Gen 45.

5) **Teach the arc** — regularly teach “creation → fall → promise → preservation.”  
Why: narrative clarity strengthens faith. Refs: Gen 1–3; Gen 12; Gen 50.

### Public (5)
1) **Resist Babel branding** — don’t build identity on self-made glory.  
Why: Babel scatters; God gathers rightly. Refs: Gen 11:4; Gen 12:2.

2) **Dignity-first leadership** — lead as a steward, not a taker.  
Why: image-bearing means stewardship. Refs: Gen 1:26–28.

3) **Bless your city** — one weekly act of practical good.  
Why: blessing is outward-facing. Refs: Gen 12:1–3.

4) **Interpret setbacks with faith** — don’t call evil good; call God faithful.  
Why: providence reshapes despair. Refs: Gen 50:20.

5) **Peacemaking habits** — seek understanding before accusation.  
Why: covenant people fight different. Refs: Gen 13:8–9; Gen 33.
